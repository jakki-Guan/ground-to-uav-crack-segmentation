## Introduction

Pavement crack segmentation is a foundational component of automated road-condition assessment, and recent segmentation models have made it increasingly practical to extract dense crack maps from inspection imagery. At the same time, UAV-assisted pavement inspection is attractive because it can increase inspection coverage, reduce traffic interference, and capture road surfaces from viewpoints that are difficult to obtain consistently from ground-based acquisition alone. In practice, however, target-domain UAV annotations are costly to collect and curate. This creates a strong incentive to reuse models trained on existing ground-view crack datasets for UAV deployment.

That reuse assumption is risky. A crack segmentation model trained on ground imagery is exposed to a different combination of scale, viewpoint, texture statistics, lighting, surface clutter, and contextual structure than it encounters in UAV imagery. As a result, a model that performs competitively on a source-domain benchmark may still fail under target-domain deployment. In this work, we treat that mismatch as a deployment domain-shift problem rather than as a purely in-domain segmentation problem. Our focus is therefore not on proposing a new architecture, but on understanding how large the transfer failure is, what kinds of errors dominate it, and which low-cost interventions are most useful when dense UAV supervision is unavailable.

A second practical issue is that deployment failure is not only a matter of missed cracks. In cross-domain settings, false positives can also become structured, high-confidence errors that overlap with visually crack-like nuisance patterns such as pavement edges, surface boundaries, shadows, and line-like texture. These errors are especially problematic for infrastructure inspection because they can produce misleading defect maps even when the underlying model appears confident. For this reason, we frame false-positive diagnosis as a first-class part of the study rather than as a minor qualitative add-on.

We study this problem using `Crack500` as the source training domain and `UAV_Crack_Segmentation_Kaggle` as the target deployment domain. The paper evaluates four settings. First, we establish source-only cross-domain baselines to quantify the zero-shot deployment gap. Second, we test a low-cost `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation strategy (`B1`), where crack-free but high-risk target-domain regions are mixed into source-domain training without directly optimizing on target-domain positive crack pixels. Third, we audit the mined banks to examine whether these negatives are genuinely useful crack-like false positives or mostly noise, and we use a nuisance taxonomy to characterize the structures that trigger the models. Fourth, we measure how efficiently few-shot target fine-tuning (`B2`) closes the remaining gap under small labeled UAV subsets.

Our experiments show that source-only transfer is not deployment-ready, even for the strongest backbone in the study. On the fixed UAV hold-out split, the source-only `SegFormer-B2` baseline reaches `IoU 0.1442`, whereas the promoted `B1` configuration improves to `IoU 0.3775`, and the `DeepLabV3+` `B1` line reaches `IoU 0.3860`. With limited target supervision, the gap closes much further: `SegFormer-B2 fs20` reaches `IoU 0.5686`, close to the frozen UAV in-domain upper bound of `IoU 0.5879`. These results indicate that the dominant challenge is not that UAV crack segmentation is intrinsically intractable, but that domain shift is severe and must be addressed explicitly.

At the same time, the mechanism behind this low-cost mitigation is more nuanced than a simple “mined negatives are always better” story. Matched random-background controls show that generic target-background exposure explains a substantial share of the gain on `SegFormer-B2`, while manual audit and curated-bank follow-up reveal that mined-bank quality remains important and model-dependent. This motivates a claim-bounded interpretation: `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation is useful as a practical deployment step, but the unique value of mined negatives depends on bank quality and backbone behavior.

The main contributions of this paper are fourfold. First, we provide a controlled benchmark of the ground-to-UAV deployment gap for crack segmentation across representative backbone families. Second, we introduce a false-positive diagnosis workflow based on qualitative audit and nuisance taxonomy, enabling a more interpretable analysis of deployment errors. Third, we evaluate a practical `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation strategy that improves deployment performance without directly optimizing on target-domain positive crack pixels. Fourth, we quantify supervision efficiency under few-shot UAV fine-tuning and show that modest target annotation recovers most of the remaining gap. Together, these contributions position ground-to-UAV crack segmentation as a deployment diagnosis problem with actionable mitigation and recovery paths.

## Problem Setting

We consider binary crack segmentation under source-to-target domain shift. Let the source domain be `Crack500`, which provides labeled ground-view pavement crack images, and let the target domain be `UAV_Crack_Segmentation_Kaggle`, which contains UAV-acquired pavement imagery with an official `train / val / test` split. The semantic label space is shared across domains, but the image formation process differs substantially due to changes in viewpoint, scale, acquisition height, scene context, and nuisance texture. Our objective is not to learn a universal domain-invariant representation in the abstract, but to evaluate practical deployment behavior when a source-trained model is reused on the UAV target domain.

We study four evaluation settings. In the **source-only** setting, a model is trained only on `Crack500` and then evaluated directly on the UAV target split. This setting quantifies the raw deployment gap and provides the main zero-shot baseline. In the **`B1` `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation** setting, we mine target-style hard negatives from UAV training imagery while excluding regions that overlap with target crack `GT` pixels. These background-only target crops are mixed into source-domain training as a lightweight way to expose the model to target-domain nuisance structures without directly optimizing on target-domain positive crack pixels. In the **`B2` few-shot** setting, we fine-tune from the source-domain checkpoint using small labeled subsets of UAV training data, specifically the frozen `fs05`, `fs10`, and `fs20` splits. Finally, in the **target upper-bound** setting, we train and evaluate directly in-domain on the UAV split to estimate the target-domain performance ceiling.

The official reporting protocol follows a validation-first, confirmatory-test rule. Model selection and threshold selection are performed on `UAV val`, while `UAV test` is used only for confirmatory hold-out reporting once a candidate has already been chosen. This rule is especially important for the `B1` setting, where multiple mined-bank configurations and thresholds are possible. In the final paper-facing tables, raw predictions remain the official main-result rows, while deployment-oriented postprocessing variants are reported separately as comparison-only operating points rather than as the default headline results.

We report `mIoU`, `F1`, `precision`, and `recall` throughout the study. `mIoU` serves as the main ranking metric, while `F1` and the precision-recall pair help characterize whether an apparent gain comes from genuine segmentation improvement or from a threshold-driven operating-point shift. This is particularly relevant in the cross-domain setting because false-positive suppression can improve precision while harming recall, and the paper aims to distinguish true mitigation from simple post-hoc calibration effects.

Three backbone families are included to separate the main storyline from supporting evidence. `SegFormer-B2` is the primary model for the headline diagnostic and mitigation study because it yields the strongest overall transfer line. `DeepLabV3+` serves as a backbone-generality support model, showing that the domain-gap and mitigation story is not confined to a single architecture family. `U-Net` is retained as a classical reference model and historical baseline, but it is not intended to dominate the main paper narrative.

## Method

### Source-only baselines

The study begins with source-only training on `Crack500` followed by direct evaluation on UAV imagery. This stage establishes the deployment gap before any target-style mitigation is introduced. We treat these raw source-only rows as the formal zero-shot reference because they most faithfully reflect natural cross-domain generalization. Deployment-style postprocessing is evaluated separately, but it is not allowed to replace the raw baseline in the main result tables.

### `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation (`B1`)

Our `B1` mitigation strategy is based on `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative exposure. Starting from a source-trained model, we run inference on UAV training images and identify high-confidence connected components that do not overlap with target crack ground truth. These components define background-only target crops that are visually risky for the model because they resemble crack-like structures or target-domain clutter. The resulting bank is then mixed into source-domain training as an auxiliary negative dataset. This procedure uses target ground truth only to exclude positive crack pixels from the mined crops; it does not directly optimize the model on target-domain positive crack pixels.

In practice, the usefulness of this strategy depends strongly on bank construction. Different mining thresholds and component-selection rules produce banks with different levels of purity, diversity, and visual relevance. The final promoted `B1` rows are therefore chosen by validation-first selection followed by confirmatory hold-out testing. For `SegFormer-B2`, the promoted row is `TS-bank thr080_mean082 @ 0.6`; for `DeepLabV3+`, the promoted row is `TS-bank area1200 @ 0.5`.

### False-positive audit and nuisance taxonomy

To understand why some mined-bank variants help while others do not, we perform a structured false-positive audit. This audit is an analysis layer rather than a new learning algorithm. It separates bank quality from bank mechanism by assigning each reviewed crop two labels. The first layer is a bank-quality judgment: `hard_fp`, `ambiguous`, `noise`, `bad_crop`, or `gt_issue`. The second layer is a nuisance taxonomy describing the visual structure that triggered the false positive, such as `pavement_edge`, `shadow_dark_stripe`, `line_like_texture`, `surface_boundary`, `debris_object`, or `other_target_clutter`.

This audit serves two purposes. First, it provides an interpretable account of what the model is confusing with cracks in the target domain. Second, it allows us to test whether visually curated subsets of the mined bank produce stronger training outcomes than the original bank. In `DeepLabV3+`, this analysis helps explain why a simple area-based rule outperforms visually stricter curated exports. In `SegFormer-B2`, the audit clarifies bank composition and shows that removing `ambiguous` examples can help on one bank variant, even though the curated reruns still do not surpass the raw mined winner.

### Matched random-background control

Because hard-negative banks increase target-domain exposure, an important question is whether the gain comes from specific mined nuisance patterns or simply from seeing more UAV background. To test this, we construct a matched random-background control in which control crops are sampled from target-domain background-only regions while avoiding crack `GT` overlap. This comparison isolates a key mechanism question: whether mined high-confidence false positives carry value beyond generic target-background exposure. The answer is not uniform across backbones, so the paper treats this as a claim-boundary analysis rather than as a one-sided confirmation of mined-bank superiority.

### Few-shot target fine-tuning (`B2`)

The few-shot setting estimates how much labeled UAV supervision is required once the deployment gap has been established. We initialize from the source-domain checkpoint and fine-tune on small labeled subsets of UAV training data using the frozen `fs05`, `fs10`, and `fs20` splits. These experiments are not intended to replace the low-cost `B1` analysis. Instead, they quantify the supervision efficiency of practical recovery: once it is clear that source-only deployment fails and `B1` only partially mitigates the problem, how quickly can limited target annotation close the remaining gap?

From an `Automation in Construction` perspective, these components form a staged deployment workflow rather than a single method claim. A practitioner can first measure source-only transfer failure on a small target validation set, then diagnose whether the dominant errors are structured false positives, then apply `GT`-filtered target-background / hard-negative mitigation as a low-cost intermediate step, and finally decide whether the remaining gap justifies target annotation for few-shot fine-tuning. In that sense, the paper's main value is not only a set of accuracy improvements, but a practical decision structure for deployment, false-positive control, and annotation-budget planning.

### Operating-point selection and reporting rule

The study enforces a strict reporting rule. Candidate models and thresholds are selected on `UAV val`, and `UAV test` is used only once a candidate is already frozen. This prevents circular tuning on the hold-out set and keeps the paper narrative focused on confirmatory evidence. It also supports a clean table design in which raw source-only rows, promoted `B1` rows, few-shot `B2` rows, and target upper-bound rows each occupy a distinct role in the final comparison.
